Behavioral Science with oTree

General Instructions

Choose one project from the list below. In a team of 2-3 people, you will design and implement a behavioral experiment using oTree, simulating data, analyzing results, and presenting your findings.

Each project must include:

  1. A functional oTree implementation: The app must run without errors and include randomized treatments.
  2. Data Simulation: A comprehensive tests.py file to simulate user behavior (bots) and verify data integrity.
  3. Data Analysis: A simple analysis of the simulated data (using R or Python).
  4. Oral Presentation: A 15-20 minute pitch and demo.

Presentation Structure:

  • Pitch (5 min): Context, client’s problem, behavioral hypothesis, and proposed solution.
  • Demo (10 min): Live demonstration of the oTree experiment (involving the class via smartphones/PCs).
  • Analysis (5 min): Basic visualization of simulated results and expected outcomes.

1. Simple Projects

1.1 The Power of Scarcity

Context: An e-commerce website wants to investigate how scarcity cues influence consumer behavior. They suspect that highlighting limited availability or high demand increases the likelihood of purchase.

Research Question: Does the type of scarcity message (quantitative vs. social) affect the click-through rate (CTR) or purchase probability?

Experimental Design: Create a product selection task where participants view items under different conditions:

Example :

  • Treatment A (Control): Neutral product display (no messages).
  • Treatment B (Quantitative Scarcity): Message indicating low stock (e.g., “Only 2 items left!”).
  • Treatment C (Social Scarcity): Message indicating high interest (e.g., “10 people are viewing this item right now”).

Deliverables:

  • oTree app with randomized treatment assignment
  • Participant instructions page.
  • Analysis of click rates/purchases across the three conditions.

1.2 Framing Effects on Subscriptions

Context: A streaming platform wants to optimize its subscription page. They need to understand how the presentation of pricing options (framing) alters user choices between monthly and yearly plans.

Research Question: How does framing the cost (e.g., emphasizing savings vs. flexibility) impact the uptake of long-term subscriptions?

Experimental Design: Design a subscription choice interface with randomized framing:

Example:

  • Treatment A (Baseline): Standard presentation (e.g., “$10/month” vs. “$100/year”).
  • Treatment B (Loss Aversion/Gain): Emphasize savings (e.g., “Yearly plan: Get 2 months free”).
  • Treatment C (Flexibility): Emphasize freedom (e.g., “Monthly plan: Cancel anytime, total flexibility”).

Deliverables:

  • oTree app collecting the user’s choice and a short follow-up on their reasoning.
  • Randomization mechanism.
  • Report comparing the proportion of “Yearly” subscriptions across treatments.

1.3 Social vs. Algorithmic Recommendations

Context: An online clothing retailer is debating its recommendation strategy. They want to know if customers trust peer choices (social proof) more than algorithmic curation.

Research Question: Do users prefer products recommended by “other customers” or products “selected for them” by an algorithm?

Experimental Design: Create a simulated shopping environment where products are highlighted based on the condition:

Example:

  • Treatment A (Social): Recommendations labeled as “Most purchased by others.”
  • Treatment B (Algorithmic): Recommendations labeled as “Selected for you by our AI.”

Deliverables:

  • Fictional product selection interface.
  • Click-stream data collection.
  • Behavioral analysis comparing the click-through rate of recommended items in both conditions.

1.4 The Influencer Trust Test

Context: A brand wants to measure the “Influencer Effect.” They need to determine if a product is perceived more favorably when endorsed by a social media personality compared to a standard neutral presentation.

Research Question: Does an influencer endorsement significantly increase trust or purchase intention compared to a neutral product description?

Experimental Design: Participants view a product profile under one of two conditions.

Example:

  • Treatment A (Neutral): Standard technical datasheet and product photo.
  • Treatment B (Influencer): An Instagram-style post featuring an influencer with a photo and a descriptive caption.

Deliverables:

  • Randomization between conditions.
  • Measurement of “Trust Score” (Likert scale) or “Willingness to Buy” (0-100 slider).
  • Comparative analysis of the scores.

1.5 The Decoy Effect

Context: An online retailer wants to nudge users toward their Premium (higher margin) product. They are considering introducing a “decoy” option to make the Premium option look more attractive.

Research Question: Does the introduction of an irrelevant “decoy” option (asymmetrically dominated) increase the share of participants choosing the Premium option?

Experimental Design: Participants choose a production in one of two conditions.

Example:

  • Treatment A (No Decoy): Choice between Basic ($10) and Premium ($30).
  • Treatment B (With Decoy): Choice between Basic ($10), Premium ($30), and Decoy ($35, or $30 with fewer features) — The decoy is clearly worse than the Premium option.

Deliverables:

  • oTree implementation of the choice task.
  • Data collection of the chosen product.
  • Analysis of the shift in “Premium” market share between Treatment A and B.

1.6 The “Green Nudge”

Context: An online supermarket aims to reduce plastic waste. They want to encourage customers to buy bulk products (packaging-free) instead of pre-packaged goods using behavioral nudges.

Research Question: Is social pressure (Social Norm) or environmental impact information (Ecological signaling) more effective in promoting bulk purchases?

Experimental Design: Participants have a budget of €20 to spend in a simulated store.

Example:

  • Treatment A (Control): Neutral display.
  • Treatment B (Social Norm): Bulk products display a badge: “80% of our customers choose this product.”
  • Treatment C (Impact): Bulk products display a badge: “Save 50g of plastic by choosing this.”

Deliverables:

  • A shopping list task implementation.
  • Calculation of the percentage of “bulk” items in the final basket.
  • Analysis comparing the effectiveness of the two nudges against the control.

1.7 Incentives and Productivity

Context: A gig-economy food delivery platform wants to increase courier activity during peak hours. They are hesitating between a guaranteed fixed bonus and a lottery-based bonus.

Research Question: Which incentive scheme generates higher effort (productivity): a small fixed piece-rate or a probabilistic large reward (with the same expected value)?

Experimental Design: Implement a Real Effort Task (e.g., counting zeros in a matrix, decoding text) lasting exactly 2 minutes.

Example:

  • Treatment A (Fixed): “You earn €0.50 per correctly solved task.”
  • Treatment B (Lottery): “Every correctly solved task gives you a 1 in 10 chance to win €5.00.”

Deliverables:

  • Real Effort Task logic (looping pages until timeout).
  • Payoff calculation logic based on the treatment.
  • Analysis of effort (number of tasks attempted/solved) and risk preferences.

1.8 Dishonesty and Declarations

Context: A car insurance company is losing money due to inflated claims. They want to test if the timing of an honesty pledge (signature) affects the magnitude of fraud.

Research Question: Does signing an honesty pledge before reporting a claim reduce dishonesty more than signing it after?

Experimental Design: Adapt the standard “Die-roll task”: Participants roll a die (physically or virtually) and report the result. Higher numbers equal higher payoffs.

Example:

  • Treatment A (Post-signature): Participant enters the result, then signs a statement at the bottom (“I certify this is accurate…”).
  • Treatment B (Pre-signature/Priming): Participant must sign the honesty pledge before the input field for the result appears.

Deliverables:

  • Implementation of the reporting interface.
  • Comparison of the distribution of reported numbers against the statistical probability (uniform distribution).
  • Analysis of “cheating magnitude” between conditions.

2. Advanced Real-Time Projects

Note: The following projects involve continuous time, multiplayer interactions, and high-frequency data. They require the use of advanced oTree features such as live_method, JavaScript (Highcharts/Bootstrap), and custom databases (ExtraModel or JSON serialization).

2.1 The Dutch Auction (Flash Sale)

Context: A last-minute concert ticket resale platform wants to test if buyers panic and pay a higher premium during a descending-price auction compared to a standard fixed-price format.

Research Question: Does a descending-price clock (Dutch Auction) increase the final sale price due to anticipated competition and panic, compared to static pricing?

Experimental Design: Create a real-time market for a single indivisible good among a group of competing buyers.

  • Treatment A (Fixed Price): Buyers can buy the ticket at a static price at any time.
  • Treatment B (Dutch Auction): The price starts at €100 and drops by €1 every second. The first player to click “Buy” wins the item at the current price. The others earn nothing.

Deliverables:

  • Server-side timer and live_method to broadcast the decreasing price.
  • Dynamic visualization of the price drop (e.g., using Highcharts).
  • Data analysis: Distribution of winning bids based on the number of competitors.

2.2 “All or Nothing” Crowdfunding

Context: A crowdfunding startup (like Kickstarter) wants to study the momentum effect of donations. They hypothesize that people donate more frequently and generously as the campaign approaches its funding threshold.

Research Question: Does the probability and amount of donations increase exponentially as the collective pool gets closer to the required threshold?

Experimental Design: Players are endowed with tokens and have 60 seconds to make continuous, free-will donations to a public project.

Rules: If the total threshold (e.g., 200 tokens) is reached before the timeout, the project is funded and yields a multiplier return to all. If not, everyone is refunded.

Deliverables:

  • live_method to aggregate donations and broadcast the new total instantly.
  • A live Bootstrap Progress Bar updating dynamically.
  • ExtraModel to record the exact timestamp and amount of every single donation.
  • Data analysis: Plotting the cumulative donation curve over time to identify end-game momentum.

2.3 The Tragedy of the Commons

Context: The Ministry of Ecological Transition wants to understand how fast a shared resource is depleted in the absence of quotas, to calibrate future extraction taxes.

Research Question: Do extraction rates accelerate (panic behavior) as the shared resource becomes visibly scarce?

Experimental Design: A shared pool (e.g., a lake) contains 500 units. During a 90-second window, players can click an “Extract” button as fast as they want. Each click earns them a personal payoff but reduces the shared pool. All players see the remaining stock decreasing in real-time.

Deliverables:

  • High-frequency live_method handling consecutive clicks.
  • “In-Memory Batching” (saving data to a dictionary in RAM before writing to the database at the end) to prevent server crashes.
  • Automatic page submission if the stock hits 0 before the timeout.
  • Data analysis: Comparing the extraction rate (clicks per second) at the beginning vs. the end of the game.

2.4 The Ranking Effect

Context: A large logistics company wants to know if displaying a live “Best Employee Leaderboard” increases overall productivity or discourages lower-performing workers.

Research Question: Does real-time relative performance feedback (ranking) increase total effort, or does it cause the lowest-ranked participants to give up?

Experimental Design: A Real-Effort Task (e.g., solving math matrices) where players earn piece-rate points.

  • Treatment A (Control): Players only see their own score.
  • Treatment B (Leaderboard): Players see a dynamically sorted Top 3 ranking of their group updated live on their screen.

Deliverables:

  • Server-side validation of answers via live_method.
  • Dynamic DOM manipulation in JavaScript to re-sort the HTML leaderboard without reloading the page.
  • Data analysis: Time-series analysis of the effort of the “last-placed” player compared to the control group.

2.5 Information Cascade

Context: An algorithmic investment fund wants to model the formation of speculative bubbles based on mimicry, even when economic fundamentals are weak.

Research Question: How often do individuals ignore their own private information to follow the observed decisions of the majority (herd behavior)?

Experimental Design: Two urns exist (A: 70% Red balls, B: 70% Blue balls). The computer secretly picks one.

  • Sequential Gameplay: Each player receives a private signal (one ball drawn) and must publicly guess the chosen urn one after the other.
  • Players waiting their turn watch the decisions of their predecessors appear live on their screen.

Deliverables:

  • Use of get_players() to manage sequential turns within a group.
  • live_method broadcasting the decision of player \(N\) to players \(N+1\), \(N+2\), etc.
  • Data analysis: Calculating the percentage of players who enter an “information cascade” (choosing contrary to their private signal).

2.6 Real-Time Decentralized Punishment

Context: An online collaborative platform (like Wikipedia) wants to evaluate if allowing users to instantly sanction “free-riders” improves overall team contributions.

Research Question: Does the threat of immediate, real-time peer punishment sustain higher cooperation levels than standard end-of-round punishment?

Experimental Design: A 60-second continuous Public Goods Game.

  • Players use a slider to adjust their contribution level live.
  • They can see others’ contributions and can click a button to pay 1 point to deduct 3 points from a specific group member at any time.

Deliverables:

  • Complex ExtraModel to track a web of interactions (Who punished whom? When? At what contribution level?).
  • Highcharts integration showing two series: the player’s contribution level and the sanctions received over time.
  • Data analysis: Econometric analysis checking if a sanction received at time \(t\) leads to a contribution increase at time \(t+1\).

2.7 The Market for Attention (Click War)

Context: A digital marketing agency running keyword auctions (like Google Ads) wants to analyze the “War of Attrition” where bidding costs time and effort rather than money.

Research Question: In a sunk-cost auction, at what point do competitors concede to an aggressive opponent?

Experimental Design: Two players fight for a single ad space. To be the “current winner,” a player must continuously mash a button. If they stop and the opponent clicks more, the opponent takes the spot. The winner is whoever holds the spot when the 90-second timer ends.

Deliverables:

  • Extremely high-frequency data collection (requires JSON serialization in a LongStringField or optimized memory batching).
  • A dynamic Bootstrap Progress Bar acting as a “Tug-of-War” visual indicator.
  • Data analysis: Survival analysis of when bots/players “give up” the click war.

2.8 Bilateral Negotiation with a Deadline

Context: A labor union wants to investigate the “Ultimatum Effect”—the tendency for negotiations to stall until the final seconds before a strike deadline.

Research Question: Does the rate of concessions (price compromises) accelerate exponentially in the final 15 seconds of a negotiation window?

Experimental Design: A 1-on-1 over-the-counter market (1 Buyer, 1 Seller). They have 2 minutes to agree on a transaction price. - They can submit and revise offers continuously. - To close the deal, one player must explicitly click “Accept” on the other’s current offer. If the timer hits zero with no accepted offer, both earn nothing.

Deliverables:

  • Custom chat/offer interface relying entirely on live_method and liveRecv.
  • Logic to instantly end the page and calculate payoffs as soon as an offer is mutually accepted.
  • Data analysis: Measuring the average concession amount (difference between consecutive offers) over time.
